## When Violations Are Found

Whether discovered through HRSA audit or self-identified, compliance violations require a structured response.

## HRSA's Enforcement Tools

| Tool | Description |
|---|---|
| Corrective Action Plan (CAP) | Required response plan with remediation steps |
| Repayment to manufacturers | Required for diversion — must repay 340B discount on non-compliant transactions |
| Program removal | Covered entity may be removed from 340B for material/repeated violations |
| Civil monetary penalties | Available for knowing and intentional violations |

## Material vs. Non-Material Violations

**Material violation** examples:
- Evidence of diversion to non-patients
- Duplicate discount billing
- Systematic failure to verify patient eligibility

**Non-material / documentation deficiency** examples:
- Slightly outdated OPAIS information
- Minor documentation gaps with no evidence of actual diversion
- Policy gaps that did not result in actual violations

## The Voluntary Disclosure Pathway

Covered entities that self-identify violations and disclose proactively to HRSA can typically:
- Work with HRSA on a repayment plan
- Implement corrective measures with HRSA's acknowledgment
- Avoid the most severe enforcement outcomes

This is strongly preferred over waiting for HRSA to discover violations through audit.

## Manufacturer-Side Enforcement

HRSA can also take action against manufacturers who:
- Charge covered entities above the ceiling price (knowing and intentional: up to $5,000/instance + repayment)
- Restrict covered entity access to 340B pricing through limitations not authorized by statute (HRSA advisory opinions, ongoing litigation)

## Audit Readiness Best Practices

1. Maintain a standing 340B compliance committee
2. Conduct annual self-audits
3. Keep OPAIS current — verify after any organizational change
4. Maintain TPA/split-billing reports for at least 3 years
5. Document all corrective actions taken
6. Train staff on 340B requirements annually
