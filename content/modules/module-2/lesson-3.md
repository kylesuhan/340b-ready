## What Is a Duplicate Discount?

A **duplicate discount** occurs when a single unit of a drug benefits from **both** a 340B discounted purchase price **and** a Medicaid manufacturer rebate.

The prohibition on duplicate discounts is codified at **42 U.S.C. § 256b(a)(5)(A)**: covered entities must not request payment from Medicaid for a drug purchased at a 340B price if doing so would result in a duplicate discount to the government.

## Why Does This Matter?

The 340B price is already deeply discounted. If that same drug unit is also subject to a Medicaid rebate (paid by the manufacturer to the state), the manufacturer effectively subsidizes the government twice for the same drug. This is prohibited.

## Medicaid Fee-for-Service (FFS)

For **Medicaid FFS** programs, the duplicate discount risk is straightforward:
- If a covered entity purchases a drug at the 340B price and then bills Medicaid FFS for that drug, a duplicate discount occurs because the state Medicaid program will also collect a manufacturer rebate.

### Prevention: Carve-Out

The most common solution is a **Medicaid carve-out**: the covered entity simply does not use 340B-purchased drugs when billing Medicaid FFS. Instead, it purchases those drugs at non-340B prices for Medicaid FFS patients.

## Medicaid Managed Care (MCO)

For **Medicaid managed care** plans, the analysis is more complex. The rules depend on whether the state has adopted a **carve-in** or **carve-out** arrangement:

| Arrangement | Description | Duplicate Discount Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Carve-Out** | 340B drugs excluded from MCO claims; no rebates on those claims | Low |
| **Carve-In** | MCO claims are subject to Medicaid rebates | High — must track and prevent |

## HRSA and State Medicaid Agency Coordination

HRSA requires covered entities and state Medicaid agencies to have mechanisms in place to prevent duplicate discounts. Many states use **exclusion files** that flag 340B NDC-level transactions so manufacturers know not to pay rebates on those claims.
