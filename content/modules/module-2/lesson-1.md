## Why the Patient Definition Matters

The 340B program only allows covered entities to use 340B-priced drugs for their **eligible patients**. Providing 340B drugs to individuals who do not qualify is called **drug diversion** — one of the two core 340B prohibitions.

## The 1996 HRSA Patient Definition

In **1996**, HRSA issued guidance establishing the criteria an individual must meet to be considered a **340B patient** of a covered entity. The guidance has not been updated but remains HRSA's operative definition.

### Three-Part Test

An individual qualifies as a 340B patient if **all three** of the following conditions are met:

**1. The covered entity has an established relationship with the individual**

The covered entity must provide the individual with health care, and the individual must rely on the covered entity for care. This relationship is documented by health records maintained by the covered entity.

**2. The individual receives health care from a provider at the covered entity**

The provider must have clinical responsibility for the patient's ongoing care. This is not merely an administrative or referral relationship — a licensed provider at the covered entity must be responsible for the patient's treatment.

**3. The covered entity maintains records of the individual's care**

The covered entity's health records, or records of the contracted health care provider, must reflect the patient's care. This requirement supports audit documentation.

## Who Does NOT Qualify?

- A person who has never been seen by a provider at the covered entity
- A person who was referred to the covered entity but did not establish care
- An employee of the covered entity (for drugs unrelated to occupational health)
- A patient seen exclusively by a provider who is not employed by or contracted with the covered entity
- A patient whose relationship with the covered entity has lapsed (fact-specific inquiry)

## Contract Pharmacy Considerations

When a covered entity uses contract pharmacies, verifying patient eligibility is more complex. The patient must still meet the three-part test — meaning the covered entity's **TPA (third-party administrator)** typically cross-references prescription data against the covered entity's patient records.
