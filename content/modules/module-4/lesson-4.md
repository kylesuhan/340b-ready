## What Is the ADR Process?

HRSA's **Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)** process provides a formal mechanism for resolving disputes between covered entities and manufacturers under the 340B program.

The ADR regulations are codified at **42 C.F.R. Part 10**.

## Who Can File?

The ADR process is **bilateral** — both parties can file:

| Filing Party | Claim Type |
|---|---|
| **Covered Entity** | Alleged overcharge by manufacturer (price above ceiling) |
| **Manufacturer** | Alleged diversion or duplicate discount by covered entity |

## ADR Panel Composition

ADR panels are composed of HRSA-selected individuals with relevant expertise. Panels make **recommendations** to the HRSA Administrator, who then issues the **final agency decision**.

> ADR panel decisions are **not** binding until the HRSA Administrator issues a final decision. That final decision is subject to judicial review under the APA.

## Filing Requirements

To file an ADR claim, the filing party must:

1. **First attempt direct resolution** with the opposing party
2. If unresolved: submit a written complaint to HRSA including:
   - Description of the dispute
   - Evidence supporting the claim
   - Documentation of good-faith attempts to resolve directly
3. HRSA reviews for completeness and convenes a panel

## Timelines

- HRSA acknowledges receipt within **30 days**
- Panel is convened and issues a recommendation within **90-120 days** (typically)
- Administrator issues final decision following panel recommendation review

## Covered Entity ADR Strategy

When using ADR:
1. Document all direct communications with the manufacturer
2. Calculate the overcharge precisely (NDC, units, dates, amounts)
3. Retain purchase records and pricing documentation
4. Use HRSA's pricing verification resources

## Why ADR Matters in the Current Landscape

The ADR process has become more significant as manufacturers have restricted contract pharmacy access. HRSA has argued that such restrictions constitute overcharging (denial of the 340B price), making ADR a vehicle for covered entities to challenge manufacturer restrictions.
