## Overview

The 340B Drug Pricing Program is a federal program that requires pharmaceutical manufacturers participating in Medicaid to sell outpatient drugs to eligible healthcare organizations—called **covered entities**—at significantly reduced prices.

These reduced prices are known as **340B ceiling prices**, and they allow safety-net providers to stretch limited federal resources, reach more eligible patients, and provide more comprehensive services.

## Statutory Basis

The 340B program was established by **Section 602 of the Veterans Health Care Act of 1992**, codified at **42 U.S.C. § 256b** of the Public Health Service Act.

The program was named after the section of the Public Health Service Act (PHSA) where it appears: Section 340B.

> **Key date:** The 340B program was signed into law on **November 4, 1992**.

## Why Was It Created?

Before 340B, Medicaid drug rebates lowered drug costs for state Medicaid programs, but other safety-net providers—such as community health centers and free clinics—did not benefit from those lower prices.

The 340B program was designed to correct this disparity by ensuring that federally-supported safety-net providers could purchase drugs at prices comparable to those available to Medicaid.

## Primary Purpose

The program's mission is to:

1. **Enable covered entities to stretch scarce federal resources** as far as possible
2. **Reach more eligible patients** and provide more comprehensive services
3. **Generate savings** that support care for underserved and low-income populations

## What 340B Is NOT

- It is **not** a grant program — covered entities must purchase drugs (at discounted prices)
- It is **not** a patient assistance program administered for individuals
- It is **not** a requirement that savings be used in any specific way (though program integrity requires the savings to support the covered entity's mission)
- It is **not** a substitute for formal legal or compliance counsel
